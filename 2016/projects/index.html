Building the Water-Resilient City of the Future

Team Lead: Imagine H2O and Weston McBride

San Francisco has some of the purest, carbon-efficient tap water in the world. 85% of our water is delivered through almost entirely gravity fed system requiring very little fossil fuel. Since the 1930’s, this resource and system has facilitated growth and provided the lifeblood to today’s innovation economy. Now, it’s under threat. By the end of 2014, California’s reservoirs were down by 11 trillion gallons. That's 880 billion 5-minute showers. Imagine H2O is working with Code for San Francisco to create a safe future for this extraordinary resource. How can SF leverage its innovation economy to help create a resilient water supply in an uncertain world? We want to build a set of solutions that can get us achieve a significant reduction in water use, and that can keep us there. Help us think about water differently.

Who Can I Vote For?' Database

Team Lead: Nick Troiano, ChangePolitics at Change.org

Every American lives in an average of 10 separate districts with elections, and has, on average, 40+ elected officials that represent them at any given time. To date, there is no place that a citizen can go to see who all their elected representatives are, or who is running for election -- even the most politically educated people in the country rarely know who their elected representatives are, nor are they actively participating in the elections that put those people into positions of power. Using a combination of advanced engineering and crowd-sourcing, we aim to create a map of our democracy that makes it possible (and easy) for citizens to see all the people who currently represent them, along with who is running for elected office, anywhere in the United States -- and we’ll make that data available to any civic technology team who can use it. At the National Day of Civic Hacking, we would like to assemble a smart and passionate team of developers, citizens, organizers, and researchers to help us lay the foundation for building this database of our democracy –– beginning right here in San Francisco.

SF in Progress

Team Lead: Matt Castillon

The San Francisco Bay Area faces a housing crisis. In January 2010, the average rent for a 1-bedroom apartment in San Francisco was $1,258/month. As of December 2014, that price is $3,002/month, a 139% increase over 4 years. Meanwhile, the median unadjusted income for a one-person household in San Francisco has only risen by 2.5% ($69,600 to $71,350). As a result the region is rapidly becoming unaffordable. Housing decisions are made at a hyperlocal level, and draw heavily on citizen feedback. Because the housing policy and planning processes are opaque, and difficult to navigate, a minority of residents hold most of the decision making power. As a result, necessary housing is delayed for years or never produced. SF in Progress is a civic engagement tool that opens the planning process to the broader public and enables a ‘silent majority’ of current and future residents that want transformative developments to occur to have a voice. It acts as a hub for housing advocates and provides education on housing policy to the public. Our initial product maps housing projects in the city’s construction pipeline. Users will be able to track these projects and their associated public hearings.

SF Social Services OpenReferral

Team Lead: April Steed and Grayson Wright

Social service and aid organizations create and maintain many different community resource directories to serve their clients. Each organization applies similar resources and effort against a shared issue, but there is no shared knowledge, and these efforts work in isolation of each other. Individuals seeking help are left to navigate confusing lists of resources that may or may not lead them to what they need. Social workers and other intermediaries must master multiple sources of competing information, making it hard to connect their clients to the relevant help. Community development initiatives end up paying for new research to understand the state of their community resources. Enter Social Services Open Referral. We have partnered with the SF Adult Probation Department to free, organize, and distribute information about the city's social services for people getting out of prison. Our immediate goals are twofold: 1) improve the useability the annual printed resource guide "Guide to Getting Out and Staying Out" 2) finish an open source database, API, and web interface for this information. Join us!

Disaster Housing Made Simple

Team Lead: Alicia Johnson, Resilience and Recovery Manager at San Francisco DEM

Finding housing for individuals and families displaced by disaster is hard work. San Francisco Emergency Management and other City Departments struggle to find open units available for those displaced by large scale fires and other emergencies every day. To help make this process easier we want to combine open data sources which track available housing units (Zillow, DataSF sources and others) with SFDEM's API-based Situational Awareness Tool (WebEOC,) to create a board (usable by all WebEOC sister sites) to help us quickly locate possible housing opportunities able to relocate those most impacted by disaster.

Gender Equality in Tech

Team Lead: Roberta Guise and Jon Erlandson

San Francisco has emerged as a global hub for tech companies, where big name firms, numerous start-ups and non-tech firms with IT needs have created a market that’s hungry for technical talent and skilled tech workers. Yet as firms jostle for scarce talent and staffing needs grow, too few women find their place in available tech jobs. In 2014, tech women in big technology firms in the San Francisco Bay Area made up a remarkably low 16%. Clearly, the opportunity for technical women is huge. FemResources is a new nonprofit dedicated to being the go-to source where female tech talent and employers can find one another. We will match technical women with employers looking for their skills and experience, in order to bring gender balance to the tech workforce. FemResources will also be a resource and services hub for tech women at all career stages. Achieving gender equality in technology is the right thing to do. It will make our community stronger because of the diverse thinking and experiences women bring to the table. And finally, tech firms and companies with IT functions have concluded they need more tech women in their workforce.
